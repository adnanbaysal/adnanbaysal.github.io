When Squares Dance in Circles

A story about a turtle that turns by ever-growing squares — and the stunning rosettes that emerge from a cubic secret hidden in plain sight.

Imagine you're holding a pen. You take a step forward, draw a line. Then you turn by some angle and take another step. Then you turn by a different angle, and step again. If the angles follow a simple pattern — say, they grow like square numbers — something extraordinary happens. The path either closes into a perfectly symmetric flower, or it drifts forever like a helix unwound onto paper.

This is the story of angle paths. It's part geometry, part number theory, and entirely beautiful. Let's begin.

Meet the Turtle

The idea of turtle graphics goes back to the 1960s and the Logo programming language. Imagine a tiny robot — a "turtle" — that lives on a flat plane. The turtle can do two things:

  • Move forward one unit, drawing a line behind it.
  • Turn by a given angle.

By repeating these two commands, the turtle traces out a path. If the turn angle is constant, you get regular polygons. But what if the turn angle changes at each step?

A twist: growing turn angles

Now let's do something different. Instead of turning by a constant angle, let's turn by n² × c at step n. That is:

  • Step 0: turn by 0² × c = 0 (start facing right)
  • Step 1: turn by 1² × c = c
  • Step 2: turn by 2² × c = 4c
  • Step 3: turn by 3² × c = 9c
  • … and so on

At each step, we accumulate these turns. The turtle's heading after N steps is the sum of all previous turns.

The Cubic Secret

Here's where it gets interesting. The sum of the first j squares has a beautiful closed form:

Square Pyramidal Number:
0² + 1² + 2² + … + (j−1)² = j(j−1)(2j−1) / 6

This is a cubic polynomial in j. So the turtle's heading after j steps is a cubic function of j — multiplied by our constant c. This single fact explains almost everything that follows.

If we write our turn constant as c = 2π × r / s for integers r and s, the heading after j steps is:

heading(j) = 2π × r × j(j−1)(2j−1) / (6s)

For the rest of this article, we'll focus on the case r = 1 and s = N. This means c = 2π/N, and the turtle accumulates turns in units of 1/N of a full circle. This choice produces the most symmetric and mathematically rich patterns.

When Do Paths Close?

For the path to close, the turtle must return to both its starting position and its starting heading. Because the turtle always takes unit steps, the closure problem reduces to understanding the heading sequence modulo a full circle.

After one full block of N steps, the turtle's heading has changed by:

Δθ = 2π × f(N) / N    where    f(N) = N(N+1)(2N+1) / 6

If Δθ is not a multiple of 2π (i.e., f(N) is not divisible by N), then each block of N steps rotates the pattern. After enough blocks, the accumulated rotation reaches a full circle and the path closes.

The condition N divides f(N) — i.e., f(N) ≡ 0 (mod N) — simplifies to:

f(N) ≡ 0 (mod N)  ⟺  6 divides (N+1)(2N+1)
This happens exactly when gcd(N, 6) = 1.

In other words:

ConditionBehaviorExample N
gcd(N,6) = 1Drifts forever; never closes5, 7, 11, 13, 17
gcd(N,6) > 1Closes into a rosette2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12

For the closing cases, the total number of steps needed to return to the initial state is:

Total steps = N × N / gcd(N, f(N) mod N)   (for N > 3)
Special cases: N = 2 needs 4 steps; N = 3 needs 9 steps.
Closure Comparison

The closure condition ties directly to a deep result in number theory. For prime N > 3, the sum we're computing is a cubic exponential sum, and the Weil bound tells us its magnitude is at most 2√N. This connects our humble turtle to cutting-edge mathematics.

The Beauty of Rosettes

When a path closes, it forms a rosette — a flower-like pattern with rotational symmetry. The number of petals is always a divisor of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, or 12.

Why 12? Two independent symmetries are at work:

  1. Rotational symmetry from the cubic formula: The denominator 6 in f(N) forces rotations to be multiples of 60°, giving 1, 2, 3, or 6 petals.
  2. Reflection symmetry from modular squares: Because k² ≡ (N−k)² (mod N), every petal is bilaterally symmetric. When combined with the rotational symmetry, this doubles some petal counts, extending the set to divisors of 12.
The dihedral group Dₚ governs the full symmetry: p rotation petals × 2 (reflections) = 2p visual sub-petals. For N=24, you get 6 rotation petals × 2 = 12 visual sub-petals.

Want to Go Deeper?

If you're mathematically inclined, here are some threads to pull:

  • The sum of squares formula j(j−1)(2j−1)/6 is a square pyramidal number. Look up the connection to Archimedes and the stacking of cannonballs.
  • The closure analysis uses the fact that f(j+N) − f(j) ≡ f(N) (mod N), which creates a geometric series factorization across blocks of N steps.
  • The reflection symmetry k² ≡ (N−k)² (mod N) is a special case of the palindrome property of squares modulo any integer.
  • For prime N, the Weil bound gives |sum| ≤ 2√N, connecting this recreational geometry to algebraic geometry and the Riemann hypothesis over finite fields.
  • The hierarchy of sub-petal splitting depends on the 2-adic valuation ν₂(N) — how many times 2 divides N. For N=24=2³×3, you get two levels of splitting.
