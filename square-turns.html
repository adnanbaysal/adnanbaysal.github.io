Square Turns

A turtle wanders an infinite plane. At every step, it turns by an amount that grows with the square of its step number. This simple rule leads to paths that merge chaos and symmetry.

1 — Introduction

Imagine a simple creature living on a blank, infinite plane. It follows this one simple instruction: step forward exactly one unit, then turn by a chosen angle. It loops this behavior until we tell it to stop.

The trajectory it traces depends entirely on how much it turns at each step. Tell it to turn by constant 90°, and it walks in a square. Tell it to always turn by 60°, and it outlines a hexagon. Instruct it to turn by a constant irrational angle, and the path will wander chaotically over a circular orbit forever, never fully repeating a step.

In computer science, this kind of agent is known as a turtle, which is studied under the turtle graphics umbrella. It's origin dates back to the 1960s. Depending on the variant, more commands may be available. For this article, we'll stick to the very restricted command set described here. Note that Mathematica's built-in AnglePath function is similar to our restricted algorithm.

We are going to give our turtle a specific turning rule and draw the paths it traces. We'll use a discrete set of turns by choosing a positive integer S, and dividing a full circle into S equal angular slices, each measuring 360°/S. Our turtle can only face one of these S directions. At step n, it advances its direction index by a certain amount (measured in these 360°/S slices) before stepping forward. We'll try different values of S to see what kind of paths we can draw.

Notice that the turtle moves very systematically, and symmetric patterns are taking shape. The orientation changes at each step are following some underlying pattern. In later sections of this article, we'll see that many values of S produce highly symmetric rosette-like patterns. We'll try to answer two fundamental questions:

  1. Will the turtle draw a closed loop-like shape, or will it wander on the plane indefinitely?
  2. What are the mathematical reasons behind the symmetries we'll about to see?

2 — The Turning Rule

Let's say we fixed some positive integer S. At step number n ∈ {0, 1, 2, ...}, the turtle turns clockwise by n² slots. We can process this slot number modulo S, because whole multiples of S correspond to no turn at all. The remainder mod S dictates the actual change in direction.

direction_slot ← (direction_slot + n²) mod S

Tracking slots instead of degrees keeps our formulas cleaner. Let's trace a small sequence where S = 6 (providing six equally spaced headings, each 60° apart). At step 0, the turn is 0² = 0. At step 1, it's 1² = 1 slot. At step 2, it leaps to 2² = 4 slots. And so on.

Step n Turn n² Turn mod 6 Cumulative slot Direction
Why squares?

We could use many other functions to drive our turtle's movements. A linear rule of turning by n slots at step n yields curves resembling the classical Euler spiral. A randomized rule produces the chaotic path of a drunkard's walk. I've found the square function a good compromise which is both very simple to describe, and also able to produce beautiful shapes. The goal of this article is to understand why this particular rule produces these patterns.

The cumulative direction slot after step n (starting from n=0) is the sum of the sequence 0² + 1² + 2² + ... + n² which is equal to the following cubic polynomial:

f(n) = n(n+1)(2n+1) 6

We'll use these two formulas throughout the article to explain the periodicities and symmetries of the paths our turtle draws.

4 — Will the Path Close or Drift?

For the vast majority of values of S, the turtle's path form a closed, circular rosette-like symmetric shape. The possible number of "petals" on these rosettes is strictly confined to 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12 — which is the set of positive divisors of 12. However, for a some specific S values, the path drifts in positive or negative X-axis direction. Use the interactive sandbox below to see the behavioral split between closing and drifting paths.

The Simple Case: gcd(S, 6) > 1

When the greatest common divisor of S and 6 is greater than 1 (meaning either 2, 3, or both divides S), the path will always close into a symmetric rosette-shape and loop periodically over that same shape. The total number of "visible" petals will be either exactly gcd(S, 6) or 2×gcd(S, 6). I'll unpack exactly why the visible petal count can appear doubled in a later section.

gcd(S, 6) > 1 ⇒ The path closes into a rosette

The Complex Case: gcd(S, 6) = 1

This scenario is far more subtle. Through visual inspection of various values of S, I could identify three distinct behaviors:

  • If S is "square-free" (meaning all of its prime factors appear with an exponent of exactly 1), the path drifts. Examples: S = 13, S = 35 (5×7), S = 77 (7×11).
  • If all the positive even powered prime factors of S happen to belong to a specific, "special" set of primes, the path also drifts. Example: S = 11×11 = 121 (where 11 is one of those special primes).
  • Conversely, if S contains an even powered prime factor that falls outside that special set, the path surprisingly closes and loops over a 1-petal shape. Example: S = 7×7 = 49.

With manual experimentation I've found that some certain prime numners are associated with drifting behavior. For example, 11, 13, 23, 37, 47, 59, 61, 71, and 73 are the first few ones. I have not yet found a complete characterization of this phenomenon, so I present it here only as an empirical observation. Searching this sub-seuence this in (OEIS) yields a few promising candidates. So that can be a good starting point for a future reasearch on this topic.

5 — Periodicity of the Moves

Let's revisit the sum-of-squares formula: f(n) = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6. It's not hard to guess that the denominator 6 should be responsible for the relation between gcd(S, 6) and graphical properties of the paths we have seen so far. To understand this relation better, let's define two core functions of our turtle's movement.

  • Let Δθ(n) = n² mod S represent the relative turn in direction slot at step n.
  • Let θ(n) = 1² + 2² + ... + n² mod S = n(n+1)(2n+1) 6 mod S represent the cumulative absolute heading slot of the turtle at step n.

As established, every slot step corresponds to a 1/S fraction of a full 360-degree turn. Therefore, the mod S operation in the definitions of both Δθ(n) and θ(n) makes sense, since any integer multiple of S is identical to making no turn at all.

Δθ(n) is periodic

Our foundational observation is that the sequence of relative orientation shifts is periodic, cycling every S steps. The arithmetic proof for this is very simple:

Δθ(n + S) = (n + S)² = n² + 2Sn + S² = n² mod S = Δθ(n) mod S

This means that after S steps, the turtle arrives at a new coordinate position and heading, but its "instruction manual" resets. From that new location, the turtle will execute the exact same sequence of relative turns, tracing an identical shape during the next S steps, simply translated and rotated in space.

This S-periodicity alone explains why we see repeating patterns in the visualizations. However, this doesn't explain why the path closes and loops over the same shape for most values of S, and why every S-step block seems to have internal reflectional symmetry. Neither of those traits is guaranteed by periodicity. We have to dig deeper.

θ(n) is periodic

Let's apply the same logic to the cumulative heading, checking θ(n + S) mod S. To do this, we expand the sum-of-squares formula: n(n + 1)(2n + 1) / 6 = (2n³ + 3n² + n) / 6. Here is a little bit algebra:

θ(n + S) = 2(n + S)³ + 3(n + S)² + (n + S) 6

= 2(n³ + 3n²S + 3nS² + S³) + 3(n² + 2nS + S²) + (n + S) 6

= 2n³ + 6n²S + 6nS² + 2S³ + 3n² + 6nS + 3S² + n + S 6

= 2n³ + 3n² + n 6 + 6n²S + 6nS² + 6nS 6 + 2S³ + 3S² + S 6

= θ(n) + S(n² + nS + n) + 2S³ + 3S² + S 6

= θ(n) + 2S³ + 3S² + S 6 mod S
= θ(n) + θ(S) mod S

This fact shows that after every block of S steps, the turtle's absolute orientation is shifted by a constant offset of θ(S) slots. Since θ(S) = 2S³ + 3S² + S 6 = S(S+1)(2S+1) 6 mod S, we can guarantee that the orientation will eventually repeat the same sequence of cumulative turns in no more than 6S steps. In reality, we can calculate the precise minimum cycle required.

To find that minimum cycle, let's dissect θ(S) a bit further. If we factor out the multiplier S, we arrive at θ(S) = S (S+1)(2S+1) 6 mod S . Let's designate the numerator of the fractional part as kS = (S+1)(2S+1). It becomes clear that we only need to evaluate the remainder of kS mod 6. Any whole integer multiples of S essentially evaporate in modulo S arithmetic. Whatever fraction remains dictates the exact minimum period (counted in S-step blocks). By checking all possible values of S mod 6, the following pattern emerges:

S mod 6 KS KS mod 6 θ(S) mod S Period GCD(S, 6)

The table confirms our earlier visual hypothesis: the cumulative orientation period aligns with GCD(S,6) × S steps.

What does this period mean for the geometry of the graphs our turtle draw? It ensures that the turtle will resume its exact initial heading after precisely k×S steps (where k is 1, 2, 3, or 6). After that point, it will periodically repeat the same sequence of k×S steps. So the turtle will draw the exact same shape, aligned without rotation, over and over. However, the previous caveat still exist: orientation match does not guarantee a return to the starting spatial coordinates. The turtle might be drawing the same shape, but displaced away from the origin by the accumulated vector of its steps. That displacement is what causes the "drifter" paths.

6 — Number of Petals

We have now established that both the closure status of the path and the number of visual petals are tied to GCD(S, 6). Let's break down how these two properties manifest physically.

Closure when GCD(S, 6) = 1

As previewed in the section When Does the Path Close?, this scenario is a mathematical edge case. For certain values of S, the path drifts along the positive or negative x-axis. For others, it forms a closed orbit, and the turtle retraces the exact same path forever.

In this state, we know θ(S) = 0. This guarantees that the heading after S steps is identical to the starting heading. If the spatial coordiantes of the turtle after S steps also happens to match the origin, we achieve a closed, 1-petal shape. If the position is offset, the graph drifts. For the sake of taxonomy, I will classify both behaviors under the "1-petal" name, aligning the petal count directly with the GCD. As stated before, exploring the arithmetic reasons why some 1-petal paths drift while others close is outside the scope of this particular exploration, but it remains an area for future research.

Closure when GCD(S, 6) > 1

This scenario is far more intuitive. Let's trace the logic without relying on heavy algebra. Imagine the turtle completes one full block of S steps, halting at some arbitrary coordinate. Assuming it hasn't landed back on the origin, it is now displaced.

Since the squared-turn rule is periodic modulo S, the second block of S steps will trace a trajectory identical to the first — except it will be rotated by the constant offset angle θ(S). Now, picture a straight line drawn from the origin to the turtle's current displaced position. If we let the turtle run for another block of S steps, it will land at a new coordinate.

Because the second S-step path is an identical (but rotated) clone of the first block, the imaginary line connecting the ending points of the first and the second S-step blocks will have the exact same length as the first imaginary line. Furthermore, since the turtle's starting heading was rotated by θ(S), this new connecting line will be rotated by that exact same angle when compared to the previous imaginary line. Ultimately, each S-step block pushes the turtle along the invisible vertices of a regular polygon. Depending on GCD(S, 6), this rotational angle will be exactly 1/2, 2/3, or 1/6 of a full turn.

  • GCD = 6 ⇒ θ(S) = S / 6 mod S This equates to 1/6 of a full rotation. The turtle will close its path after 6S steps, yielding a six-petal hexagonal rosette.
  • GCD = 3 ⇒ θ(S) = 2S / 3 mod S This is a 2/3 rotation (or effectively a 1/3 rotation in reverse direction). The path closes after 3S steps, forming a three-petal equilateral triangle structure.
  • GCD = 2 ⇒ θ(S) = S / 2 mod S This is exactly a 1/2 rotation. The turtle closes the loop after 2S steps, producing a two-petal linear symmetry.

You might be wondering: if the math dictates only 1, 2, 3, or 6 rotational petals, why did earlier images feature 12 or even 4 petal-like structures? To answer that, we have to look closer at the internal architecture of the petals themselves.

7 — Origin of Reflection Symmetries

The previous section accounts for the top-level, rotational symmetries of our rosettes. This holds true regardless of the petal count, even applying to the 1-petal shapes. But zoom in on a single petal, and you'll notice it's not a random walk. The petal is highly structured and has reflection symmetry. Let's trace the algebraic roots of this bilateral symmetry.

Δθ(n) is palindromic

The orientation shift function, Δθ(n), holds an interesting property: it is palindromic inside every S-step block. Reading the sequence of turns forward yields the exact same list as reading it backward. The proof requires only a single line of algebra:

Δθ(S - n) = (S − n)² = S² - 2Sn + n² ≡ n² (mod S) = Δθ(n)

This means the turn executed at step n is the same as the the turn executed at step S − n. In a S-step block, the first turn matches the last, the second matches the penultimate, and so forth.

To be precise, there is a slight caveat to the block's palindromic nature. When analyzing a complete S-step block, it only forms a palindrome if we omit the very first rotation step, where the turtle turns by 0² = 0 slots. To maintain the symmetry of this sequence of numbers, we must either drop this initial zero, or append an extra zero-slot turn to the tail of the sequence. Observe the symmetry in the table below for S = 6:

The table demonstrates that discarding the initial 0 makes the sequence palindromic, but leaves us with S - 1 turns. Interestingly, this leading zero is the thing that forces the path to be reflectionally symmetric, as we'll see below.

Petal Reflection Symmetry

A geometric path consisting of S straight segments contains exactly S-1 internal joints (the corners between the lines). If the sequence of angles dictating these S-1 joints forms a palindrome, the resulting path will be reflection symmetric. The initial zero turn merely dictates the entry rotation of the entire shape.

Imagine our turtle resting at a coordinate after a sequence of steps, aligned to some cumulative heading. It begins a new S-step sequence with a zero. It steps forward, effectively changing nothing about its heading. Then, it hits the core palindromic sequence of S - 1 turns, rotating and stepping forward for each, until the sequence completes.

Now, freeze the turtle at its final position and force it to retrace its steps backward. To do this, the turtle must first flip its heading 180 degrees, then step forward. At the first joint it encounters on the journey back, it must execute the inverse of the turn it made on the way forward. If Δθn represents the n-th internal turn, the palindromic nature guarantees that Δθn = ΔθS - n. Comparing the forward and backward instruction sets looks like this:

Forward: Δθ1, Δθ2, …, ΔθS - 1
Backward: -ΔθS-1, -ΔθS-2, …, -Δθ1

Applying the palindrome identity to the backward sequence simplifies it:

Backward: -Δθ1, -Δθ2, …, -ΔθS - 1

Therefore, the turtle retracing its steps mathematically executes the exact same sequence of turn magnitudes, in the exact same order, just with negative signs. If the forward-moving turtle turns k slots clockwise at a joint, the backward-moving turtle turns k slots counter-clockwise at that corresponding joint. This is the definition of a mirror image. Since the path is inherently identical to its own mirror image, the shape itself must possess bilateral symmetry.

8 — Doubling of the Petal Count

Let's look at S = 24. We know from our previous proof that each petal (a block of 24 steps) inherently possesses bilateral symmetry. Yet, upon closer inspection, each major petal appears physically fractured into two smaller sub-petals, each with its own internal symmetry. What causes this?

The Second Shift Identity

The palindrome property (x² ≡ (S−x)²) is responsible for the petal's outer mirror symmetry. But, for some S there is a second modular identity in the arithmetic, responsible for the sub-petal cloning:

(x + S/2)² ≡ x² (mod S) if and only if 4 divides S

The proof is a simple modular algebra: (x + S/2)² = x² + Sx + S²/4. The middle term, Sx, is a clean multiple of S, so it vanishes mod S. The final term, S²/4, vanishes mod S if and only if 4 is a perfect divisor of S. ∎

Translated to geometry: whenever S is divisible by 4, the squared angular shift applied at step n is mathematically identical to the shift applied at step n + S/2. The second half of the petal has the exact same set of relative turns as the first half, just executed from a new origin point and rotation.

To put it plainly: the second half of every petal is a rotated clone of the first half. Two congruent sub-petals, each bilaterally symmetry. This is why S=24 (divisible by 4) visibly fractures into 12 lobes, while S=6 (not divisible by 4) remains visually fused as 6 solid petals.

Summary: The Architecture of a Petal

Outer symmetry (The primary mirror line): Universally true. Derived from the palindrome identity x² ≡ (S−x)².

Sub-petal splitting (The congruent cellular division): Exclusively requires 4 | S. Derived from the shift identity (x+S/2)² ≡ x².

Visual count ≈ 2 × rotational count This visual doubling occurs whenever profound bilateral symmetry effectively splits the appearance of {2, 6} petals into {4, 12} lobes.

This explains the petal counts observed in the examples considered here. You would assume the counts map strictly to the divisors of 6: {1, 2, 3, 6}. However, bilateral cloning visually doubles the {2, 6} geometries into {4, 12}. The 3-petal geometries retain their count (the symmetry exists, but visually presents as a single, broad leaf). Therefore, the observable set of visual petals is {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12} — the divisors of 12.

9 — Related and Future Work

The results discussed here — closure conditions, petal counts, and bilateral symmetry — can be placed within a broader mathematical framework: the theory of exponential sums.

If you treat each step's directional heading as a complex number positioned on the unit circle, the geometric position after n steps is nothing more than the partial sum:

P(n) = Σj=0M−1e2πi·θ(n)/S

Because θ(n) is a cubic polynomial, this is technically an exponential sum with a cubic polynomial phase. The academic study of these sums is vast, with roots going back to the work of Gauss and Weil. While specific families of cubic polynomials are well documented in the literature, to the best of my knowledge, there is little published analysis focusing explicitly on why the geometric graphs of these partial sums consistently manifest these specific, beautiful rosettes.

The closest parallel exploration I have found is a piece called Exponential Sums by David Angell. He utilizes alternate cubic polynomials to generate similarly striking visual graphs. To keep this analysis accessible, I focused on a simplified polynomial defined by a quadratic change per step rule.

Future Directions

This geometric playground is ripe with open questions:

  • Can formal exponential sum theory be deployed to analytically lock down the closure properties, symmetries, and petal generation with absolute certainty?
  • Do all cubic polynomials intrinsically generate equivalent symmetries, or is this rule special? How does the number of petals depend on the coefficients of these polynomial used?
  • What happens when we escalate the math? Can similar geometric guarantees be proven for polynomials of higher degrees?
  • What is the precise arithmetic mechanism that forces certain 1-petal configurations to close while condemning others to endless drift?
  • Why drifting graphs only drift on the X-axis? That means, when the oriantation resets after 1 block, the turtle returns to a spatial location with x=0. What determines the drifting direction? For some S, the graph drifts to the negative direction, and for others, drifts to the positive.
  • Beyond rotational and reflectional symmetry, what other geometric properties define these shapes?
  • It is entirely possible to introduce an auxiliary integer R (where GCD(R,S) = 1) and process R S full turns instead of 1 S turns as base slots. The set of all possible turns will be the same, but the visuals generated will be significantly different. In fact, the interactive sandbox linked above natively supports both R and S inputs. Analyzing the structural impact of R remains another frontier for future research.
  • What would a random walk with algebraic properties similar to the squared turn rule would look like? For example, we can create a random S-step palindromic sequence of various multiples of 1/S full turns. Besides, we can make their sum mod S to be a divisor of S, so that we can chose the petal count. This analysis would help identify what other graphical properties squared turn rule has, when compared to similar random walks.
  • Can we link the theory of Dihedral groups to our algorithm to understand the shapes we see here better?
