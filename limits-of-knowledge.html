Limits of Our Knowledge

Two kinds of knowledge

We can roughly group human knowledge into two categories:

  • External knowledge: claims about the world outside our minds.
  • Internal knowledge: structures we build and explore in thought.

Science is primarily concerned with external knowledge [2]"Mathematics Is Not Science" (Stanford University). Mathematics, and much of philosophy, are more concerned with internal knowledge. The two are deeply connected, because internal results often become tools for understanding the external world. A purely mathematical insight can later prove essential in a physical theory, as concepts developed independently of physics frequently find unexpected applications in the natural sciences [3]"The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics..." (Eugene Wigner, 1960).

A key point, though, is that internal claims can be true within their own system even if the external world turns out to behave differently.

How we generate knowledge

Whether we are doing mathematics or science, our reasoning usually follows the same broad pattern:

  1. Adopt starting assumptions (axioms, postulates, or working hypotheses).
  2. Choose rules of inference (how we are allowed to reason from one statement to another).
  3. Derive new statements by applying those rules step by step.

This description fits mathematics very clearly, but both inductive and deductive reasoning are also fundamental to scientific research [2]"Mathematics Is Not Science" (Stanford University). Since modern science is expressed through mathematical models, it also inherits much of this logical structure.

The major difference appears in the next step: verification.

Verification in mathematics

In mathematics, verification is built into the derivation itself, as theorems are rigorously proven through pure reasoning based on established rules [2]"Mathematics Is Not Science" (Stanford University).

A mathematical statement is considered established once a mathematician produces a proof that starts from accepted axioms and applies accepted inference rules. When a proof is correct, the conclusion is as certain as the underlying system allows. The result may not feel “obvious” at first, but once the proof is understood, its correctness is not a matter of further testing.

Verification in science

Science also uses reasoning and derivation, but it relies on an additional layer of validation:

  • Observation
  • Experiment
  • Measurement

The foundation of science lies in these empirical observations [2]"Mathematics Is Not Science" (Stanford University). Here, verification is never purely logical, making science inherently empirical and falsifiable [2]"Mathematics Is Not Science" (Stanford University). Measurements can be wrong. Instruments can drift. Experimental setups can contain hidden biases. So scientific knowledge includes an unavoidable element of uncertainty [1]"The Limits of Science..." (Oxford Academic).

There is also a deeper, theoretical worry: verifying a measurement method often requires another measurement, which itself needs verification, and so on. In principle, this chain has no perfectly final endpoint.

In practice, however, science does something sensible: it treats measurement as good enough for current purposes, and then improves it over time. As instruments and methods get better, scientific conclusions are refined, corrected, or sometimes replaced, because there is always a chance a better explanation will be found to match real-world observations [2]"Mathematics Is Not Science" (Stanford University).

What this means for the limits of knowledge

Both mathematics and science ultimately depend on:

  • the assumptions we start with, and
  • the rules we use to derive consequences.

In mathematics, many different consistent sets of axioms can generate valid “worlds” of internal truth. Their validity is logical: if the system is consistent, results derived within it are relative to that system. An important caveat comes from Gödel's second incompleteness theorem: a consistent formal system cannot demonstrate its own consistency [4]"Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems" (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy), so we cannot be sure if there can be contradictory results.

In science, we aim for claims that hold in the external world. Empirical testing gives science a powerful anchor to reality, but also makes its conclusions inherently provisional, as any theory must be verified by empirical observation and remains subject to falsification [2]"Mathematics Is Not Science" (Stanford University).

To make these two kinds of knowledge support each other, we tend to develop mathematical frameworks that are especially useful for modeling and predicting the external world. Mathematics supplies precise internal structures, and science supplies feedback from reality about which structures are most fruitful.
