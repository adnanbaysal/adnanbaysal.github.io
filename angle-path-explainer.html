Square Turns

A turtle walks. At every step, it turns — not by a fixed angle, but by an amount that grows with the square of the step count . What emerges is surprisingly astonishing.

01 — The Setup

The Walking Rule

Imagine a very obedient creature. It lives on a blank plane and follows one instruction: walk forward one step, then turn by an angle of your wish. That's all it ever does.

The path it traces depends entirely on how much it turns at each step. Turn always by 90°, and you get a perfect square. Turn by 60°, and you get a hexagon. Turn by some irrational angle, and the path wanders forever without passing on the same step again.

In the programming world, this kind of creature is called a turtle — it carries its direction with it and updates it at every step. We're going to give our turtle a very specific turning rule, and watch what happens.

To make the turns well-defined, we'll divide the full circle into S equal angle slices , each of size 360°/S. Our turtle can only face one of these N directions. At step number n , the turtle adds some amount to its current direction index (measured in those 360°/S slices), then steps forward.

Notice how the turtle doesn't move randomly — there's a clear pattern forming. Each step is the same length (one unit), but the direction changes systematically. The question that will drive our entire exploration is: will the turtle ever return to exactly where it started, facing the same way?

02 — The Rule

Turning by Squares

Here's the specific rule we'll use. At step number n (starting from n=1 ), the turtle increases its direction slot by (n-1)² , modulo N.

direction_slot ← (direction_slot + (n−1)²) mod N

Let's trace through a small example with N = 6 (six equally spaced directions, each 60° apart). At step 1, the turn is 0² = 0. At step 2, it's 1² = 1 slot. At step 3, it's 2² = 4 slots. And so on.

Step n Turn (n−1)² Turn mod 6 Cumulative slot Direction

The key phrase is modulo N . Once the slot index reaches N, it wraps back to 0. With N=6, the slot is always in {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5}, corresponding to directions {0°, 60°, 120°, 180°, 240°, 300°}.

Why squares?

Many functions could drive the turning — the linear choice gives spirals, the random choice gives a drunk walk. Squares sit in a beautiful middle ground: growing fast enough to create variety, but structured enough to create symmetry . This is what we're here to explore.

There's a classical result lurking here. The cumulative slot after exactly j steps is 0² + 1² + 2² + ... + (j−1)² , which equals the famous sum-of-squares formula :

f(j) = j(j−1)(2j−1) / 6

This seemingly innocent formula is the key to everything. The "6" in the denominator is not a coincidence — it will come back to haunt us in the most beautiful way.

04 — The Mystery

When Does the Path Close?

Not all choices of N produce the beautiful closed rosettes above. For some N, the path drifts endlessly , never returning to the start. Try N = 5 or N = 7 in the demo below — the path spirals outward, block after block.

The Answer: gcd(N, 6)

The closing condition turns out to be elegantly simple. Compute the greatest common divisor of N and 6. If it's greater than 1, the path closes. If it's 1, the path drifts forever.

Path closes ⟺ gcd(N, 6) > 1

In plain English: the path closes if and only if N shares a factor with 6. Since 6 = 2 × 3, this means N must be divisible by 2, by 3, or by both.

Why gcd with 6?

Look back at the sum-of-squares formula: f(j) = j(j+1)(2j+1)/6 . That denominator 6 is doing the work. Whether the path's phase shift after N steps is zero (mod N) depends on how N interacts with this 6. We'll see the full proof shortly.

Why Does It Close? The Block Structure

Here's the key insight, without heavy algebra. After the walker completes one block of N steps , it arrives at some position in the plane. Whether it has returned to the start depends on the vector sum of all N unit steps taken.

Because the squared-turn rule is periodic modulo N in a specific sense, the second block of N steps is identical to the first — but rotated by a fixed angle Δθ determined by f(N) mod N . The third block is the same shape, rotated again by Δθ, and so on.

Think of each N-step block as one "blade" of a fan. The blades are all the same shape, just rotated. The path closes when the blades complete a full rotation — i.e., when enough rotations of Δθ add up to 360°.

Δθ = 360° × f(N) / N (mod 360°)

For the path to close, Δθ must be a rational fraction of 360°. It turns out that Δθ is always a multiple of 60° — and this is precisely because of the 6 in the denominator of f. More on this next.

05 — The Petal Count

Counting Petals — Divisors of 6

Look at the table above. The petal counts are 2, 3, and 6 — and for drifting paths, we could say there's "1 petal" that just repeats endlessly. Those four numbers — 1, 2, 3, 6 — are exactly the divisors of 6 .

This is no accident. Here's the reasoning in full.

The rotation angle between consecutive blocks is Δθ = 360° × (f(N) mod N) / N . Let's think about what values this can take.

We have f(N) = N(N+1)(2N+1)/6 , so f(N)/N = (N+1)(2N+1)/6 . The denominator is always 6 (before cancellation with the numerator). So f(N)/N is always a multiple of 1/6, which means Δθ is always a multiple of 360°/6 = 60° .

The Petal Theorem

Δθ ∈ {0°, 60°, 120°, 180°, 240°, 300°}. The number of rotation petals is 360° / Δθ , which is always a divisor of 6: either 1, 2, 3, or 6 .

Which divisor of 6 you get depends precisely on gcd(N, 6):

  • gcd = 1   Δθ = 0°, the block repeats identically → path drifts forever
  • gcd = 2   Δθ = 180° → 2 petals (each a 180° rotation of the other)
  • gcd = 3   Δθ = 240° → 3 petals (each a 120° rotation)
  • gcd = 6   Δθ = 60° → 6 petals (each a 60° rotation)

06 — The Symmetry

The Hidden Symmetry

Look carefully at a single petal. It's not just one blob — it has a mirror line running through its middle. Both halves of the petal are reflections of each other.

This bilateral symmetry comes from one of the most elegant modular-arithmetic identities you'll ever see:

x² ≡ (N − x)² (mod N)

Why? Expand the right side: (N−x)² = N² − 2Nx + x² . Both N² and −2Nx are multiples of N, so they vanish modulo N, leaving x² . ✓

What does this mean for our turtle? It says that the turn at step n is identical to the turn at step N − n , modulo N. In other words: the sequence of turns in each N-step block is palindromic . The first turn equals the last, the second equals the second-to-last, and so on.

A palindromic sequence of directions means the path traces out and then retraces in a mirrored fashion — which is exactly what bilateral symmetry means for a curve. This is true for every single N , not just special cases.

The Exact Identity

There's an even stronger result. Define the cumulative sum f(j) = 1² + 2² + ··· + j² . Then for any N and any step j:

f(j−1) + f(N−j) ≡ f(N−1) (mod N)

The sum of cumulative phases at symmetric steps is always the same constant. This constant defines the angle of the mirror axis of each petal.

So every petal has a bilateral symmetry. Now, 2 petals with bilateral symmetry look like 4 lobes. 6 petals with bilateral symmetry look like 12 lobes. That's the mystery of 12 solved — but there's an even deeper level to this story.

07 — Going Deeper

The Mystery of 12 Petals

For N = 24, each petal (block of 24 steps) already has bilateral symmetry. But when you look closely, each petal seems to consist of two smaller sub-petals , each with their own bilateral symmetry. Why?

The Second Shift Identity

The palindrome property ( x² ≡ (N−x)² ) gives each petal its outer bilateral symmetry. But there's a second modular identity that creates the sub-petal structure:

(x + N/2)² ≡ x² (mod N) if and only if 4 divides N

Proof: (x + N/2)² = x² + Nx + N²/4 . The term Nx vanishes mod N. The term N²/4 vanishes mod N if and only if 4 divides N. ∎

What this says is: when 4 divides N, the squared increments at step j and at step j + N/2 are equal modulo N . The second half of each petal has the same sequence of directions as the first half — just starting from a rotated position.

In other words: the second half of each petal is a rotated copy of the first half . Two congruent halves, each itself bilaterally symmetric. That's why N=24 (divisible by 4) shows sub-petal structure, while N=6 (not divisible by 4) doesn't.

Summary: What Creates Petal Structure

Outer symmetry (each petal has a mirror line): Always true. Comes from x² ≡ (N−x)² .

Sub-petal splitting (the petal has two congruent halves): Requires 4 | N. Comes from (x+N/2)² ≡ x² .

Visual petal count ≈ 2 × rotation petals when bilateral symmetry makes each petal look like two lobes.

This explains the full story of petal counts. You expect divisors of 6: {2, 3, 6}. But bilateral symmetry can visually double {2, 6} → {4, 12}. And 3-petal shapes retain 3 (the bilateral symmetry is present but each petal looks like a single elongated leaf). So the observable counts are {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12} — precisely the divisors of 12.

Why Divisors of 12?

The rotation symmetry (from the 6 in the denominator) gives divisors of 6. The reflection symmetry (from x² ≡ (N−x)²) adds a factor of 2, extending the range to divisors of 12. The denominator 6 = 2 × 3, and reflection adds another factor of 2, giving 12 = 2 × 6 as the "full symmetry group" size.

08 — The Deep End

The Deeper Connection

Everything we've discovered — the closing condition, the petal count, the bilateral symmetry — can be unified under a single mathematical framework: exponential sums .

If you think of each step's direction as a complex number on the unit circle, the partial sum after J steps is:

S(J) = Σ j=0 J−1 e 2πi·f(j)/N

This is an exponential sum with cubic polynomial phase — because f(j) is a cubic polynomial in j. The study of such sums is deep, going back to Gauss and Weil.

Our key results translate directly:

  • The block structure : S(mN) = S N × (geometric series in ρ), where ρ = e^(2πi·f(N)/N).
  • The closing condition : The path closes iff the geometric series terminates — i.e., ρ is a root of unity of finite order, i.e., gcd(N,6) > 1.
  • The Weil bound : For prime N not dividing 6, |S N | ≤ 2√N. This is why even the drifting paths have bounded "error" per block — they drift slowly relative to the number of steps.
Weil's Theorem (1948)

For any prime N > 3 and any cubic polynomial with nonzero leading coefficient, the corresponding exponential sum is bounded in absolute value by 2√N. This bound is essentially tight — beautiful shapes live right at the edge of this bound.

The Weil bound tells us something visually striking: for large prime N (which gives drifting paths), the walker's displacement after N steps has magnitude at most 2√N. The walker takes N unit steps but only makes net progress of order √N — it's almost random, yet not quite.

Future Directions

Many natural questions remain open for exploration:

  • Can we use exponential sum theory to compute the exact shape of the petals analytically?
  • What happens with other polynomial turning functions — cubics, quartics?
  • The closing condition gcd(N,6) > 1 depended on the 6 in the denominator of the sum-of-squares formula. What closing conditions arise from other polynomial sums?
  • The bilateral petal symmetry came from x² ≡ (N−x)². Does every even-degree polynomial turning function produce this symmetry?
