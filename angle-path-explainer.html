Square Turns

A turtle walks. At every step, it turns — not by a fixed angle, but by an amount that grows with the square of the step count. What emerges is surprisingly beautiful.

01 — The Setup

The Walking Rule

Imagine a very obedient creature. It lives on a blank plane and follows one instruction: walk forward one step, then turn by an angle of your wish. That's all it ever does.

The path it traces depends entirely on how much it turns at each step. Turn always by 90°, and you get a perfect square. Turn by 60°, and you get a hexagon. Turn by some irrational angle, and the path wanders forever without passing on the same step again.

In the programming world, this kind of creature is called a turtle — it carries its direction with it and updates it at every step. We're going to give our turtle a very specific turning rule, and watch what happens.

To make the turns well-defined, we'll divide the full circle into S equal angle slices, each of size 360°/S, for varying values of S. Our turtle can only face one of these S directions. At step number n, the turtle adds some amount to its current direction index (measured in those 360°/S slices), then steps forward.

Notice how the turtle doesn't move randomly — there's a clear pattern forming. Each step is the same length (one unit), but the direction changes systematically. The question that will drive our main exploration is: will the turtle ever return to exactly where it started, facing the same way? When these two conditions met, we'll get a periodic graph.

02 — The Rule

Turning by Squares

Here's the specific rule we'll use. At step number n (starting from n=0), the turtle increases its direction slot by n². Note that we can calculate this slot number modulo S, since whole multiples of S will correspond that many full cycles, and only the remainder is necessary to calculate direction slot change.

direction_slot ← (direction_slot + n²) mod S

Using slots instead of degrees or radians makes the formulas cleaner. Let's trace through a small example with S = 6 (six equally spaced directions, each 60° apart). At step 1, the turn is 0² = 0. At step 2, it's 1² = 1 slot. At step 3, it's 2² = 4 slots. And so on.

Step n Turn (n−1)² Turn mod 6 Cumulative slot Direction

The key phrase is modulo S. Once the slot index reaches S, it wraps back to 0. With S=6, the slot is always in {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5}, corresponding to directions {0°, 60°, 120°, 180°, 240°, 300°}.

Why squares?

Many functions could drive the turning — the linear choice (turn by n slots in the step n) gives graphs that resemble the Euler spiral, the random choice gives a drunk walk. Squares sit in a beautiful middle ground: growing fast enough to create variety, but structured enough to create symmetry. This is why we're here to explore.

There's a classical result lurking here. The cumulative slot of direction after exactly n steps is 0² + 1² + 2² + ... + (n−1)² , which equals the famous sum-of-squares formula :

f(n) = n(n−1)(2n−1) / 6

The relation between the cubic cumulative direction formula and quadratic direction change per step formula is the key to the chaotic symmetries we'll explore in the rest of this article.

04 — The Mystery

When Does the Path Close?

For most of the values of S, the graph of the path perfectly closes in a circular way after some number of steps. That's why we call the resulting shape a rosette. The possible number of petals of the rosette are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12. These are exactly the positive divisors of 12. On the other hand, some S result in a path that drifts endlessly that never returning to the start, but instead proceeds in either the positive or the negative x-axis direction forever. When gcd(S, 6) > 1

This is the easier case. When 2, or 3, or both divides S, the path always closes in a rosette like shape and repeates periodically after that. In this case, number of petals is either gcd(S, 6) or 2 x gcd(S, 6). In any case, the path closes after exactly S x gcd(S, 6) steps. For simplicty, I'll call a single consecutive S steps as a "block". I'll come to why visible number of petals can be double the value of gcd later.

gcd(S, 6) > 1 ⇒ path closes in a rosette shape

When gcd(S, 6) = 1

This case is more complicated. Visually analyzing the behavior for some small S values, I discovered following:

  • If S is square-free (all prime factors has power 1), the graph drifts. For example: S = 35 = 5x7, S = 77 = 7x11
  • If all even powered prime factors of S belong to a special set of primes, then the graph drifts. For example S = 11x11 = 121
  • Otherwise, if S has an even powered prime factor that is out of the above set of primes, then the graph closes with a 1 petal shape. For example S = 7x7 = 49
Experimentally, I found that the following primes belongs to the special set mentioned above: 11, 13, 23, 37, 47, 59, 61, 71, 73, ... This set is probably infinite, and a search on oeis.org give 4 potential candidates for this sequence. Honestly, I don't know which one of those sequences' definition match the best for the underlying reason of this result. That would be a good future work.

05 — The Algebra

Why GCD With 6?

Let's look back at the sum-of-squares formula: f(n) = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6 . That denominator 6 seems to be related to this. To understand this better, let's make a few observations about the formulas for relative slot change and cumulative direction formulas. Before proceeding to deal with the formulas, let's give name to the orientation change, and cumulative orientation formulas.

  • Let Δθ(n) = n² denote the amount of direction slot change at step n of the walk.
  • Let θ(n) = 1² + 2² + ... + n² = n(n+1)(2n+1) / 6 denote the cumulative direction slot at step n of the walk.

As before, each slot corresponds to 1/S of a full turn. Therefore, we only need to consider Δθ(n) mod S and θ(n) mod S as full turns correspond to no turn at all.

Δθ(n) is periodic

Our first observation is that the amount of relative orientation changes is periodic with a period of S steps. The following simple observation shows this:

Δθ(n + S) = (n + S)² = n² + 2Sn + S² = n² mod S = Δθ(n)

Now let's think what this means geometrically. After S steps of walk, the turtle is positioned at some location in the plane and has an orientation that is the sum of all changes up to that point. The turtle has so far already drawn a graph. Thinking of this last position and orientation as the starting state, it will draw exactly the same shape in a rotated and translated manner in the next S steps.

So it's clear that this single S-periodicity of Δθ(n) is enough to explain the repeating rotated+translated patterns in our graphs. But there are more than that in what we saw in the graphs. For example, as we saw, the path closes for almost all choices of S. Besides, each S-step block has reflection symmetry too, which cannot be explained by this periodicity alone. We'll see the reasons in the following sections.

θ(n) is periodic

just like we did for Δθ(n + S), let's check θ(n + S) to see if we get S-periodicity. To make working with formulas easier, we'll use the expanded form of the sum of squares formula: n(n + 1)(2n + 1) / 6 = (2n³ + 3n² + n) / 6. Here we go:

θ(n + S) = 2(n + S)³ + 3(n + S)² + (n + S) 6

= 2(n³ + 3n²S + 3nS² + S³) + 3(n² + 2nS + S²) + (n + S) 6

= 2n³ + 6n²S + 6nS² + 2S³ + 3n² + 6nS + 3S² + n + S 6

= 2n³ + 3n² + n 6 + 6n²S + 6nS² + 6nS 6 + 2S³ + 3S² + S 6

= θ(n) + S(n² + nS + n) + 2S³ + 3S² + S 6

= θ(n) + 2S³ + 3S² + S 6 mod S
= θ(n) + θ(S) mod S

The above identities show that after each block of S-step, the turtle's orientation is rotated by θ(S) mod S slots. Since θ(S) = 2S³ + 3S² + S 6 = S(S+1)(2S+1) 6 , it's guaranteed that the orientation returns back to the original orientation (facing positive x-axis) after at most 6S steps. In fact, we can do better, and find the exact minimum period for cumulative orientations to repeat.

To understand that, let's analyze θ(S) mod S in more detail: We know that θ(S) is an integer since its the sum of first S squares. If we seperate the multiplier S in the formula, we get θ(S) mod S = S (S+1)(2S+1) 6 mod S. If we call kS = (S+1)(2S+1), we see that we can calculate θ(S) mod S by just considering the remainder of kS mod 6, because integer multiples of S will be equivalent to 0 mod S. Whatever that value is, we'll get a fraction of S, which will help to find the minimal period as the number of S-step blocks. For that, we just need to check possible values of S mod 6:

S mod 6 KS KS mod 6 θ(S) mod S Period GCD(S, 6)

In the above table, we showed θ(S) mod S as a fraction of S to clearly see the period. Periad is simple calculated as the minimum multiple of this fraction to make it an integer multiple of S. The table shows that the period for cumulative orientation is exactly GCD(S,6) × 6 as we guessed.

So, again, what does kS-periodicity (k is one of 1, 2, 3, or 8) mean geometrically for the cumulative orientation slot of the turtle? It means, the turtle will face the exact same orientation after kS-steps. Since Δθ(n) is also S-periodic, this means the turtle will draw the same shape in the same orientation, i.e. not a rotated version. But there is an important catch here, the turtle does not have to return to the beginning position. It could have draw the same shape starting from another position in the space, and that position is the sum of all steps as 2D vectors. That would cause the graphs to drift forever in some direction, but we do not see this except for some specific family of choices of S. So there is certainly more math to analyze.

06 — The Petal Count

Counting Petals — Divisors of 6

As we saw before, number of closure of the graphs and the number of petals are directly related to GCD(S, 6). We'll analyze these two properties in this section.

Closure when GCD(S, 6) = 1

As we saw in the section When Does the Path Close?, this case has intricacies. For some S, the path drifts in the positive x-axis direction. For others, it forms a closed shape and the turtle moves over the same closed graph forever.

For this case, we have θ(S) = 0 mod S, so the orientation after S-steps is the same as the starting orientation. When the position of turtle after S-steps is also the starting position, we'll get a 1-petal closing graph. When the position is different, the graph drifts in some orientation. I'll call both of these cases as a 1-petal shape, to make talking about petals and GCD(S, 6) the same thing. I'll also leave analyzing when and why graph drifts vs closes as future work, to keep the scope of this article short.

Closure when GCD(S, 6) > 1

This case is easier to understand. Here's the key insight, without heavy algebra. After the walker completes one block of S-steps, it arrives at some position in the plane. It may or may not returned to the starting position, which depends on the vector sum of all S unit steps taken. If returned to the initial position, then we are good: we get a periodically closing graph. So let's assume that turtle does not return to the original position after S-steps.

Because the squared-turn rule is periodic modulo S , the second block of S steps is identical to the first — but rotated by the fixed angle θ(S) mod S . The turtle is now at a position in plane that is different from the origin. Now, assume we draw a line from the origin to this new position. If we proceed one more block of S-steps, we get to another position in space. Since the turtle draws exactly the same shape at each S-step blocks, this new line will have the same length as the first one. Besides, since turtle's orientation is rotated by θ(S) mod S slots, this new line will also be rotated by the same amount. Hence, each S-steps block will move the turtle on the edges of some equally angled eqaul length lines. Depending on GCD(S, 6), the angle of rotation is one of 1/2, 2/3, or 1/6 of full cycle.

  • GCD = 6 ⇒ θ(S) = S / 6 mod S By the definition of θ, this is 1/6 of full cycle. In this case, Turtle closes the path after 6S steps in a six-petal hexagonal shape.
  • GCD = 3 ⇒ θ(S) = 2S / 3 mod S This is 2/3 of full cycle, which is infact 1/3 of full cycle in counter clockwise direction. In this case, Turtle closes the path after 3S steps in a three-petal equilateral triangle shape.
  • GCD = 2 ⇒ θ(S) = S / 2 mod S This will correspond to 1/2 of full cycle. In this case, turtle closes the path after 2S steps in a two-petal linear shape.

07 — The Symmetry

The Hidden Symmetry

The descriptions in the previous section explains the rotational symmetries found the in the graphs we have seen so far. And this is true for all number of petals, including 1 petal, which has 0 rotational symmetry. But when we look at a single petal carefully, we see that it's not just one blob — it has a mirror line running through its middle. Both halves of the petal are reflections of each other. In this section, we'll try to understand where that comes from.

Δθ(n) is palindromic

There is a nice property of the orientation change function Δθ(n) mod S: it's palindromic, meaning you get the same sequence of orientation changes if you start from the beginning or end. This also means that these slot change values has reflection symmetry. This is easily proved below:

Δθ(S - n) = (S − n)² = S² - 2Sn + n² ≡ n² (mod S) = Δθ(n) mod S

What does this mean for our turtle geometrically? It says that the turn at step n is identical to the turn at step S − n. In other words: the sequence of turns in each S-step block is palindromic. The first turn equals the last, the second equals the second-to-last, and so on.

Actually, I lied a bit in the above descriptions. When we consider a full S-step block, it's only palindromic if we omit the initial step in which the turtle turns 0² = 0 slots. We need to either remove this 0, or add an extra step with a 0 slot turn at the end to make the sequence palindromic. To understand what I mean, look at the table below for S = 6:

n n2 n2 mod 6
0 0 0
1 1 1
2 4 4
3 9 3
4 16 4
5 25 1
6 36 0
…

In the above table, we see that we need to discard the initial 0s to make a block of S = 6 turn amounts palindromic. But in that case we would have S - 1 turns instead of S. But we'll see below that this extra 0 actually makes the petal has reflection symmetric!

Proof of petal reflection symmetry

To see why, first realize that a path with S-lines contains S-1 internal joints. Each internal joint has a corresponding turn amount, so there are actually S-1 internal turns in a block of S-steps. If this internal turn amounts are palindromic, we claim that the corresponding path will be reflection symmetric. In fact, the leading 0 could be any value, it would just rotate the remaining path.

Assume our turtle has some cumulative orientation at some step in the drawing that is a multiple of S. At the beginning, that cumulative orientation is 0, that is positive x-axis, but it could be an other value. Turtle has a command of S turns that start with 0, then a palindromic sequance of S - 1 turns. Now, let the turtle complete that S step. It will first step forward in the current inherited orientation since the first turn is 0, which means no turn at all. Then it will turn by the first value in the sequence that is part of palindromic sub sequence and move one step forward. This way, it will go till the end, make the last turn and step forward.

Now, being at the final position, let's make our turtle trace back the path it traversed. As a first step, we need to revert the final orientation of the turtle, then step forward in that direction without turning. After that, the turtle needs to turn the same amount as the last internal joint, but in the reverse direction. In the algebra of rotations, this is the negative of the last turn. Let Δθn denote the n-th internal turn for n=1, 2, … , S - 1. So we have Δθn = ΔθS - n because the subsequence is palindromic. So the forward and backward internal turn sequences are as follows:

Forward: Δθ1, Δθ2, …, ΔθS - 1
Backward: -ΔθS-1, -ΔθS-2, …, -Δθ1

If we substitute palindromic values in the backward sequence, we get the following:

Backward: -Δθ1, -Δθ2, …, -ΔθS - 1

So, the backward path has exactly the same turn amounts in the same order that are negated. So if forward turtle turns k slots clockwise at step n, the bacward turtle turns k slots counter clockwise at that same step. If you would put a mirror to the plane and draw an path with the turtle, the mirror image will exactly do the same: It will have negated turn amounts. Since this is an intrinsic property of this sequence of turns, it means the path will have reflection symmetry!

08 — Going Deeper

The Mystery of 12 Petals

For S = 24, each petal (block of 24 steps) already has bilateral symmetry. But when you look closely, each petal seems to consist of two smaller sub-petals , each with their own bilateral symmetry. Why?

The Second Shift Identity

The palindrome property ( x² ≡ (S−x)² ) gives each petal its outer bilateral symmetry. But there's a second modular identity that creates the sub-petal structure:

(x + S/2)² ≡ x² (mod S) if and only if 4 divides S

Proof: (x + S/2)² = x² + Sx + S²/4 . The term Sx vanishes mod S. The term S²/4 vanishes mod S if and only if 4 divides S. ∎

What this says is: when 4 divides N, the squared increments at step n and at step n + S/2 are equal modulo S . The second half of each petal has the same sequence of directions as the first half — just starting from a rotated position.

In other words: the second half of each petal is a rotated copy of the first half . Two congruent halves, each itself bilaterally symmetric. That's why S=24 (divisible by 4) shows sub-petal structure, while S=6 (not divisible by 4) doesn't.

Summary: What Creates Petal Structure

Outer symmetry (each petal has a mirror line): Always true. Comes from x² ≡ (S−x)² .

Sub-petal splitting (the petal has two congruent halves): Requires 4 | S. Comes from (x+S/2)² ≡ x² .

Visual petal count ≈ 2 × rotation petals when bilateral symmetry makes each petal look like two lobes.

This explains the full story of petal counts. You expect divisors of 6: {2, 3, 6}. But bilateral symmetry can visually double {2, 6} → {4, 12}. And 3-petal shapes retain 3 (the bilateral symmetry is present but each petal looks like a single elongated leaf). So the observable counts are {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12} — precisely the divisors of 12.

09 — Next Steps

Related and Future Work

Everything we've discovered — the closing condition, the petal count, the bilateral symmetry — can be unified under a single mathematical framework: exponential sums .

If you think of each step's direction as a complex number on the unit circle, the partial sum after n steps is:

P(n) = Σj=0M−1e2πi·θ(n)/S

This is an exponential sum with cubic polynomial phase — because θ(n) is a cubic polynomial in n. The study of such sums is deep, going back to Gauss and Weil. There are some research articles that related to what we show here. Even some specific family of cubic polynomials studied in the literature, but afaik, there is no work with detailed analysis of the reasons why these graph of partial exponential sums give these beautiful shapes.

The closest work to This article that I can find is Exponential sums by David Angell. In that work, he used different cubic polynomials and they seem to create similarly intricate and beautiful graphs. For the ease of anaylsis, I've based my analysis on a simpler and fixed polynomial which is in fact defined by it's quadratic "change per step" function.

Future Directions

Many natural questions remain open for exploration:

  • Can we use exponential sum theory to prove the closure properties, symmetries, and number of petals analytically?
  • Do all cubic polynomials produce similar symmetries?
  • What about polynomials with different degrees? Can we prove similar properties for them?
  • What is the exact reason some 1-petal graphs close and some drift?
  • What are the other geometric properties of the shapes besides rotational and reflectional symmetries?
